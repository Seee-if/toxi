1. Cardiac Arrest

Description: Cardiac arrest is a sudden and unexpected loss of heart function, leading to a halt in blood circulation and oxygen delivery to vital organs. This condition can result in death within minutes if not treated immediately.

Causes:

  • Coronary artery disease (blockage in heart arteries).
  • Heart attack (myocardial infarction).
  • Arrhythmias (e.g., ventricular fibrillation or tachycardia).
  • Cardiomyopathy (weak heart muscle).
  • Drug overdose or poisoning.
  • Severe trauma or injury.
  • Electrolyte imbalances (e.g., high potassium levels).

Symptoms:

  • Sudden collapse or fainting.
  • No detectable pulse.
  • No breathing or gasping breaths.
  • Unconsciousness.
  • Cyanosis (bluish discoloration of lips or skin).

Key Body Changes:

  • Heart Rate (HR): Completely absent or highly irregular.
  • Blood Pressure (BP): Rapid drop to zero or unmeasurable.
  • Oxygen Saturation (SpO₂): Declines rapidly to 0%.

Detection Devices:

  • Pulse Oximeter: Detects absence of pulse and oxygen saturation.
  • ECG (Electrocardiogram): Identifies arrhythmias or flatline activity (asystole).
  • AED (Automated External Defibrillator): Helps detect and treat shockable arrhythmias.

Detection Time:

Immediate; symptoms and device readings provide near-instant information (seconds).

How to Save a Person After Detection:

  1. Recognize the Emergency: Confirm cardiac arrest (check responsiveness, breathing, and pulse).
  2. Call for Help: Dial emergency services (e.g., 911).
  3. Start CPR: Perform high-quality chest compressions.
  4. Use an AED: Turn the AED on and follow voice instructions.
  5. Continue Care Until Professional Help Arrives: Monitor the victim for any signs of recovery.

2. Stroke

Description:

A stroke occurs when the brain's blood supply is interrupted, leading to oxygen deprivation and brain tissue damage. It is a medical emergency that requires immediate attention to minimize permanent damage or death.

Types of Stroke:

  1. Ischemic Stroke:
    • Cause: Blockage in a blood vessel supplying the brain, often due to a blood clot or atherosclerosis.
    • Prevalence: Accounts for ~87% of all strokes.
  2. Hemorrhagic Stroke:
    • Cause: Rupture of a blood vessel, causing bleeding in or around the brain.
    • Risk Factors: High blood pressure, trauma, aneurysms.
  3. Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA):
    • Cause: Temporary blockage of blood flow to the brain.
    • Duration: Symptoms resolve within 24 hours, often a warning sign of future strokes.

Symptoms (FAST):

  • F: Face drooping (one side).
  • A: Arm weakness or numbness (usually one-sided).
  • S: Speech difficulty (slurred, incomprehensible).
  • T: Time to call emergency services (stroke is an emergency).

Additional symptoms:

  • Vision problems (blurred or double vision).
  • Sudden confusion or trouble understanding.
  • Severe headache (common in hemorrhagic stroke).

Key Body Changes:

  1. Blood Pressure (BP):
    • Elevated systolic BP (>180 mmHg) in hemorrhagic stroke.
    • May remain normal or mildly elevated in ischemic stroke.
  2. Mental Status (GCS - Glasgow Coma Scale):
    • Reduction in GCS score (<12 indicates moderate to severe brain dysfunction).
    • Confusion, drowsiness, or loss of consciousness.
  3. Capillary Refill Time (CRT):
    • Delayed (>2 seconds), especially in ischemic cases due to poor circulation.

Detection Devices:

  • BP Monitor: Identifies hypertension, which is often associated with stroke.
  • Clinical Assessments: FAST tests, neurological exams, and GCS scoring.
  • Imaging (for confirmation):
    • CT Scan: Differentiates between ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke.
    • MRI: Provides detailed images to identify affected areas.

Detection Time:

Varies depending on stroke type:

  • TIA: Detection within minutes (symptoms resolve quickly).
  • Ischemic Stroke: Can take minutes to hours.
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke: Often detected rapidly due to severe symptoms like headache and vomiting.

How to Save a Person After Detection:

  1. Call Emergency Services: Time is critical; prompt treatment minimizes brain damage.
  2. Positioning: Lay the person down with their head elevated at ~30 degrees to reduce intracranial pressure. Turn their head to one side to prevent choking if vomiting occurs.
  3. Monitor Vital Signs: Check breathing, pulse, and level of consciousness. Start CPR if there is no pulse or breathing.
  4. Do NOT Offer Food or Drinks: Swallowing may be impaired, increasing the risk of aspiration.

Hospital Treatment:

  1. For Ischemic Stroke:
    • Thrombolysis: Administer tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) within 4.5 hours of symptom onset to dissolve clots.
    • Mechanical Thrombectomy: Removal of clots using a catheter for large artery blockages (within 6–24 hours).
  2. For Hemorrhagic Stroke:
    • Blood Pressure Control: IV medications to lower BP and prevent further bleeding.
    • Surgical Intervention: Repair ruptured blood vessels (e.g., clipping an aneurysm).
    • Draining Hematomas: Removal of collected blood to relieve pressure on the brain.

Advanced Monitoring and Support:

  • Neurological Monitoring: Continuous assessment of GCS and reflexes.
  • Rehabilitation:
    • Physical therapy to regain motor skills.
    • Speech therapy for language deficits.
    • Psychological counseling for emotional support.

Importance of Time:

For every minute without treatment, ~2 million brain cells die. Acting fast and providing appropriate care dramatically improves survival and recovery.

3. Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Description:

A pulmonary embolism (PE) occurs when a blood clot, typically originating from a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the leg, travels through the bloodstream to the lungs and obstructs blood flow in the pulmonary arteries. This blockage disrupts the exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide, leading to reduced oxygen levels in the blood and potential damage to lung tissue. The severity of a PE can range from mild to massive, with massive PEs causing a sudden drop in blood pressure and cardiac arrest.

Causes:

  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Prolonged immobility (e.g., long flights, bed rest).
  • Recent surgery or trauma (especially to the legs).
  • Cancer, obesity, and smoking.
  • Pregnancy or estrogen-containing medications.
  • Inherited clotting disorders (e.g., Factor V Leiden).
  • Heart conditions like atrial fibrillation.

Symptoms:

  • Sudden shortness of breath.
  • Sharp or stabbing chest pain, worsening with deep breaths.
  • Rapid heart rate (tachycardia).
  • Cough (sometimes with blood-stained sputum).
  • Anxiety, lightheadedness, sweating, or fainting.
  • Leg pain or swelling (if associated with a DVT).

Key Body Changes:

  • Low oxygen saturation (SpO₂ < 90%).
  • Increased respiratory rate (>20 breaths/min).
  • Low end-tidal CO₂ (EtCO₂ < 35 mmHg).
  • Elevated D-dimer and troponin levels.

Detection Devices:

  • Pulse Oximeter: Monitors oxygen saturation.
  • Respiratory Monitor: Tracks respiratory rate and patterns.
  • Capnography: Measures end-tidal CO₂.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Assesses heart rhythm and right heart strain.
  • Imaging Studies:
    • CT Pulmonary Angiography (gold standard for diagnosis).
    • Ventilation/Perfusion (V/Q) scan.
    • Pulmonary Angiography.

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures):

  • Oxygen Therapy: Supplemental oxygen to improve blood oxygen levels.
  • Anticoagulation: Immediate use of heparin or low molecular weight heparin to prevent further clots, followed by oral anticoagulants.
  • Thrombolytic Therapy: For massive PE with hemodynamic instability, clot-busting drugs like alteplase may be administered.
  • Embolectomy: Surgical or catheter-directed clot removal for severe cases.
  • IVC Filter: A filter in the inferior vena cava to prevent future clots from reaching the lungs, for patients who cannot use anticoagulants.
  • Supportive Care: Monitoring vital signs, managing pain and anxiety, and ensuring adequate hydration.

4. Aortic Dissection

Description:

An aortic dissection is a serious condition in which a tear develops in the inner lining of the aorta, the large blood vessel branching off the heart. This tear creates a false channel within the aortic wall, allowing blood to flow between the layers. This can weaken the aorta, potentially leading to rupture and life-threatening internal bleeding. Dissections are classified by their location within the aorta:

  • Type A: Involves the ascending aorta.
  • Type B: Involves the descending aorta.

Causes:

  • Chronic high blood pressure (hypertension).
  • Genetic connective tissue disorders (e.g., Marfan syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome).
  • Bicuspid aortic valve (a congenital heart defect).
  • Trauma (especially blunt chest trauma).
  • Cocaine use.
  • Certain pregnancy complications.

Symptoms:

  • Sudden, severe chest or back pain, often described as a ripping or tearing sensation.
  • Pain radiating to the neck, jaw, back, or abdomen.
  • Fainting or dizziness.
  • Shortness of breath.
  • Weakness or numbness in an arm or leg (due to impaired blood flow).
  • Stroke-like symptoms (if blood flow to the brain is affected).

Key Body Changes:

  • Blood pressure may initially be elevated but can drop dramatically if the aorta ruptures.
  • Widened pulse pressure (difference between systolic and diastolic BP).
  • Rapid heart rate (tachycardia).
  • Imaging studies will show the characteristic double lumen of the dissected aorta.

Detection Devices:

  • Blood Pressure Monitor: Detects fluctuations in blood pressure.
  • Imaging Studies:
    • CT Angiography (gold standard).
    • MRI.
    • Transesophageal Echocardiography (TEE).
    • Chest X-ray.

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures):

  • Blood Pressure Control: Rapidly lower BP with IV medications (e.g., beta-blockers like esmolol or labetalol).
  • Pain Management: Aggressive pain control to reduce stress and anxiety.
  • Type A Dissection Treatment:
    • Requires emergency surgery to repair the tear and prevent rupture.
    • Damaged section of the aorta is replaced with a synthetic graft.
  • Type B Dissection Treatment:
    • Managed medically with blood pressure control and close monitoring.
    • Endovascular repair or open surgery for complications like rupture or uncontrolled pain.
  • Long-Term Management:
    • Control blood pressure with medication and lifestyle modifications.
    • Regular follow-up imaging to monitor the aorta for further dissection or aneurysm formation.

5. Anaphylaxis

Description:

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction that occurs rapidly after exposure to an allergen (a substance that triggers an allergic response). The reaction involves the release of histamine and other chemicals from mast cells and basophils, causing widespread inflammation and affecting multiple organ systems. Airway swelling, circulatory collapse (shock), and respiratory distress are major concerns.

Causes:

  • Foods (e.g., peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, milk, eggs, soy, wheat).
  • Insect stings (e.g., bees, wasps, hornets, fire ants).
  • Medications (e.g., penicillin, other antibiotics, aspirin, NSAIDs).
  • Latex.
  • Certain diagnostic or therapeutic agents (e.g., radiocontrast dyes).
  • Idiopathic (specific trigger cannot be identified).

Symptoms:

  • Difficulty breathing (wheezing, shortness of breath, tightness in the chest).
  • Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, and throat (angioedema).
  • Hives or rash (itchy, red welts on the skin).
  • Sudden drop in blood pressure (hypotension).
  • Dizziness or fainting.
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps.
  • A sense of impending doom.

Key Body Changes:

  • Rapid increase in heart rate (tachycardia, >100 bpm).
  • Severe hypotension (blood pressure <90/60 mmHg).
  • Decline in oxygen saturation (SpO₂) due to airway obstruction and/or shock.
  • Wheezing or stridor (high-pitched breathing sounds indicating airway narrowing).

Detection Devices:

  • Pulse Oximetry: Monitors oxygen levels.
  • Blood Pressure Monitor: Detects hypotension.
  • Blood Tests: Measure tryptase levels (marker of mast cell activation).

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures):

  • Epinephrine (Adrenaline) Administration: Intramuscular injection is the first-line and most important treatment for anaphylaxis. It counteracts histamine's effects by constricting blood vessels, raising blood pressure, relaxing airway muscles, and reducing swelling. Multiple doses may be necessary.
  • Supplemental Oxygen: High-flow oxygen is administered to maintain adequate oxygenation.
  • Intravenous Fluids: Rapid infusion of normal saline to increase blood volume and support blood pressure.
  • Antihistamines: H1 blockers (diphenhydramine) and H2 blockers (ranitidine, famotidine) to reduce histamine effects and alleviate symptoms like itching and hives.
  • Corticosteroids: Help reduce inflammation and prevent a biphasic reaction (recurrence of symptoms several hours after the initial episode).
  • Airway Management: Intubation may be necessary for severe airway obstruction.
  • Observation: Patients should be monitored for several hours after an anaphylactic reaction to detect potential biphasic reactions.

6. Sepsis

Description:

Sepsis is a life-threatening condition that arises when the body's response to an infection becomes dysregulated and damages its own tissues and organs. It's characterized by systemic inflammation, which can lead to organ dysfunction, circulatory collapse (septic shock), and ultimately death. Sepsis is a medical emergency requiring prompt recognition and aggressive treatment.

Causes:

  • Bacterial, viral, fungal, or parasitic infections.
  • Common infection sites: lungs (pneumonia), urinary tract (UTIs), abdomen (appendicitis, peritonitis), skin (cellulitis), and bloodstream (bacteremia).
  • Increased risk: weakened immune systems, chronic illnesses, invasive medical devices.

Symptoms:

  • Early signs: fever or low body temperature (hypothermia), rapid heart rate (tachycardia), rapid respiratory rate (tachypnea), altered mental state (confusion, disorientation).
  • Advanced signs: low blood pressure (hypotension), difficulty breathing, reduced urine output, mottled or discolored skin, organ dysfunction (e.g., kidney failure, liver failure, respiratory failure).

Key Body Changes:

  • Fever (>38°C/100.4°F) or hypothermia (<36°C/96.8°F).
  • Tachycardia (>100 bpm) and tachypnea (>20 breaths/min).
  • Hypotension (systolic BP <90 mmHg or a drop of >40 mmHg from baseline).
  • Elevated white blood cell count, C-reactive protein (CRP), and procalcitonin (PCT) levels.
  • Abnormal blood clotting tests; blood cultures may identify the causative organism.

Detection Devices:

  • Thermometers: Measure body temperature.
  • Pulse Oximetry: Monitors oxygen saturation.
  • Blood Pressure Monitors: Detects hypotension.
  • Laboratory Tests: Complete blood count, blood cultures, CRP, PCT, blood gases, coagulation studies.
  • Imaging Studies: X-ray, CT scan, ultrasound to identify the source of infection.

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures):

  • Fluid Resuscitation: Rapid IV administration of crystalloids (normal saline or lactated Ringer's solution) to restore blood volume and improve blood pressure.
  • Antibiotics: Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics intravenously as soon as possible after drawing blood cultures. Refine antibiotic choice based on identified organisms and sensitivities.
  • Vasopressors: If blood pressure remains low, administer vasopressor medications (e.g., norepinephrine, dopamine, vasopressin) to constrict blood vessels and raise blood pressure.
  • Source Control: Identify and treat the source of infection (e.g., draining abscesses, removing infected tissue).
  • Respiratory Support: Provide supplemental oxygen or mechanical ventilation if respiratory function is compromised.
  • Supportive Care: Monitor vital signs, manage pain and anxiety, optimize blood glucose levels, and provide nutritional support.

7. Sudden Arrhythmic Death Syndrome (SADS)

Description:

Sudden arrhythmic death syndrome (SADS) refers to sudden cardiac death caused by an undiagnosed heart rhythm abnormality (arrhythmia), typically in young, apparently healthy individuals. These arrhythmias can disrupt the heart's electrical system, leading to ventricular fibrillation (a chaotic, ineffective heart rhythm) and cardiac arrest.

Causes:

  • Inherited genetic heart conditions, such as:
    • Long QT syndrome.
    • Brugada syndrome.
    • Catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (CPVT).
    • Inherited cardiomyopathies (diseases of the heart muscle).
  • Sometimes, the underlying cause cannot be identified.

Symptoms:

  • Often asymptomatic; sudden cardiac arrest may be the first and only sign.
  • Possible warning signs:
    • Fainting episodes (syncope).
    • Palpitations (a sensation of rapid or irregular heartbeat).
    • Seizures or chest pain.
    • Family history of sudden cardiac death or unexplained fainting.

Key Body Changes:

  • During cardiac arrest:
    • Heart rate becomes irregular or absent.
    • Blood pressure drops to zero.
    • Oxygen saturation rapidly declines.
  • An ECG during an arrhythmia event shows the specific abnormal heart rhythm.

Detection Devices:

  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Detects arrhythmias.
  • Genetic Testing: Identifies inherited conditions associated with SADS.
  • Implantable Cardiac Monitors: Continuously record heart rhythms and detect life-threatening arrhythmias.

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures):

  • Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR): Immediate CPR is crucial to maintain circulation and oxygenation until advanced medical help arrives.
  • Defibrillation: Use an AED or manual defibrillator to restore a normal heart rhythm if ventricular fibrillation is present.
  • Advanced Cardiac Life Support (ACLS): Protocols include medications like epinephrine, amiodarone, and magnesium to manage cardiac arrest and stabilize the patient.

Long-Term Management:

  • Medications: Antiarrhythmic drugs, beta-blockers.
  • Implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs): Prevent sudden cardiac death.
  • Lifestyle modifications and regular follow-ups with a cardiologist specializing in inherited heart rhythm disorders.
  • Genetic counseling and screening for family members.

8. Trauma

Description:

Trauma encompasses physical injuries caused by external forces, such as accidents, falls, assaults, or natural disasters. Trauma can range from minor injuries to severe, life-threatening conditions involving internal bleeding, organ damage, fractures, and spinal cord injuries. Rapid assessment and intervention are critical to minimize morbidity and mortality.

Causes:

  • Motor vehicle accidents.
  • Falls.
  • Gunshot wounds and stab wounds.
  • Blunt force trauma (e.g., assaults, sports injuries).
  • Burns and crush injuries.
  • Natural disasters (e.g., earthquakes, hurricanes).

Symptoms:

  • Pain and swelling at the injury site.
  • Visible wounds or deformities.
  • Bleeding (external or internal).
  • Shock (pale, cool, clammy skin; rapid heart rate; low blood pressure).
  • Difficulty breathing.
  • Loss of consciousness or altered mental state.
  • Paralysis or numbness (spinal cord injuries).

Key Body Changes:

  • Blood pressure drops due to blood loss or shock.
  • Heart rate increases (tachycardia) as a compensatory response.
  • Respiratory rate may increase due to pain or chest injury.
  • Oxygen saturation decreases if respiratory compromise or shock occurs.
  • Blood tests may show anemia, abnormal clotting, or elevated lactate levels (indicating tissue damage).

Detection Devices:

  • Blood Pressure Monitor: Detects hypotension.
  • Pulse Oximetry: Monitors oxygen saturation.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Assesses heart rhythm.
  • Imaging Studies:
    • X-ray and CT scan for fractures and internal injuries.
    • Ultrasound for internal bleeding (FAST: focused assessment with sonography for trauma).
    • Angiography to detect vascular injuries.

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures):

  • Airway Management: Clear obstructions, provide oxygen, or perform intubation if necessary.
  • Breathing Support: Provide mechanical ventilation or chest tubes to drain air or blood from the chest cavity.
  • Circulation Support:
    • Stop bleeding with direct pressure, tourniquets, or surgical intervention.
    • Administer intravenous fluids (crystalloids, colloids, or blood products) to restore blood volume.
  • Disability Assessment: Perform a neurological evaluation to check for head or spinal cord injuries; immobilize the spine as needed.
  • Exposure and Environmental Control: Expose the injury site fully while preventing hypothermia.
  • Pain Management: Administer pain relief medication to improve comfort and reduce stress.
  • Definitive Care: Surgery to repair damaged organs, stop bleeding, or stabilize fractures.

9. Epileptic Seizures

Description:

Epileptic seizures are episodes of abnormal, excessive electrical discharges in the brain that disrupt normal brain function. These discharges can cause a variety of symptoms, including convulsions (jerking movements), loss of consciousness, changes in sensation, and altered behavior. Seizures can vary in type, severity, and duration.

Causes:

  • Epilepsy, a neurological disorder characterized by recurrent seizures.
  • Brain injuries or trauma.
  • Infections (e.g., meningitis, encephalitis).
  • Brain tumors or stroke.
  • Genetic conditions or metabolic disturbances.
  • Drug withdrawal or substance abuse.
  • Idiopathic (cause unknown).

Symptoms:

  • Generalized seizures: Involve the entire brain and may cause convulsions, muscle rigidity, and loss of consciousness.
  • Focal seizures: Originate in a specific brain area and may result in localized symptoms like twitching or sensory changes.
  • Absence seizures: Brief episodes of staring and unresponsiveness.

Key Body Changes:

  • Brain activity shows abnormal spikes and waves during a seizure (measured by EEG).
  • Heart rate may increase during the event.
  • Oxygen saturation may decrease if breathing is impaired.
  • Postictal state: confusion, drowsiness, or muscle soreness after the seizure.

Detection Devices:

  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): Detects abnormal brain activity.
  • Imaging Studies: MRI or CT scan can identify structural abnormalities in the brain.
  • Wearable Seizure Detectors: Can alert caregivers, especially for nocturnal seizures.

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures & Ongoing Management):

During a Seizure:

  • Protect the individual from injury (e.g., move furniture, cushion the head).
  • Do not restrain the person or put anything in their mouth.
  • Time the seizure and roll the person onto their side to maintain a clear airway.
  • Call emergency services if the seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes or if it's the person's first seizure.

Long-Term Management:

  • Medications: Antiepileptic drugs (AEDs) are the mainstay of treatment to reduce or eliminate seizures.
  • Epilepsy Surgery: For cases where seizures are not controlled by medication and originate from a specific brain area.
  • Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS): A device implanted to deliver electrical pulses to the vagus nerve.
  • Ketogenic Diet: A high-fat, low-carbohydrate diet that can reduce seizure frequency in some individuals.
  • Lifestyle Modifications: Adequate sleep, avoiding known triggers, and abstaining from alcohol and drugs.

10. Drug Overdose

Description:

A drug overdose occurs when an excessive amount of a drug is taken, leading to toxic effects on the body. Overdoses can involve prescription medications, over-the-counter drugs, or illicit substances. The effects of an overdose can range from mild to severe and can be life-threatening, especially when respiratory or cardiac function is compromised.

Causes:

  • Accidental overdose.
  • Intentional overdose (suicide attempt).
  • Substance use disorder (addiction).
  • Polydrug overdoses (involving multiple substances).
  • Common drugs involved:
    • Opioids (e.g., heroin, fentanyl, prescription painkillers).
    • Sedatives (e.g., benzodiazepines, barbiturates).
    • Stimulants (e.g., cocaine, amphetamines).
    • Alcohol.

Symptoms:

  • Slow or stopped breathing (respiratory depression).
  • Pinpoint pupils (opioid overdose).
  • Unconsciousness or extreme drowsiness.
  • Confusion or seizures.
  • Nausea, vomiting, and chest pain.
  • Irregular heartbeat or cardiac arrest.

Key Body Changes:

  • Respiratory rate slows (bradypnea <12 breaths/min) or stops (apnea).
  • End-tidal CO₂ levels increase (hypercapnia >45 mmHg) due to respiratory depression.
  • Oxygen saturation declines (SpO₂).
  • Heart rate and blood pressure abnormalities, depending on the drug involved.
  • Blood tests can detect specific drugs and assess organ function.

Detection Devices:

  • Pulse Oximetry: Monitors oxygen levels.
  • Respiratory Monitors: Track breathing rate and patterns.
  • Capnography: Measures end-tidal CO₂ levels.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Assesses heart rhythm.
  • Blood Tests: Toxicology screens, arterial blood gases, and liver/kidney function tests.

Treatment (Life-Saving Measures):

  • Airway Management: Establish a clear airway using manual maneuvers or airway adjuncts; intubation may be required for respiratory arrest.
  • Respiratory Support: Provide supplemental oxygen; administer naloxone (Narcan) for opioid overdoses to reverse respiratory depression.
  • Circulation Support: Administer IV fluids to maintain blood pressure; medications may address specific drug effects.
  • Gastric Lavage: May be performed in some cases to remove unabsorbed drugs from the stomach.
  • Symptomatic Treatment: Manage seizures with anticonvulsants and treat other symptoms (e.g., nausea, chest pain) as needed.

Key Indicators

1. Heart Rate (HR)

Value Description: The number of heartbeats per minute, indicating heart rhythm and rate.

Tools to Measure:

  • Photoplethysmography (PPG): Found in smartwatches and fitness trackers.
  • Chest Strap Monitors: Provide more precise HR readings.
  • Pulse Oximeters: Measures HR alongside oxygen saturation.

2. Oxygen Saturation (SpO₂)

Value Description: Percentage of oxygen bound to hemoglobin in the blood, reflecting oxygen delivery efficiency.

Tools to Measure:

  • Pulse Oximeters: Standard devices for SpO₂ and HR monitoring.
  • Wearable Devices: Smartwatches and ring monitors with SpO₂ sensors.

3. Blood Pressure (BP)

Value Description: The force of circulating blood against artery walls, affecting cardiac workload.

Tools to Measure:

  • Traditional BP Monitors (Sphygmomanometers): Includes cuffs and manual or automatic monitors.
  • Cuffless BP Devices: Use algorithms to estimate BP from pulse wave velocity or other measurements.

4. Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

Value Description: The variation in time intervals between heartbeats, indicating autonomic nervous system activity.

Tools to Measure:

  • ECG Monitors: Most accurate for HRV measurement.
  • PPG-Based Wearables: Devices like smartwatches estimate HRV indirectly.

5. Respiratory Rate

Value Description: Number of breaths per minute, often linked to oxygenation and heart function.

Tools to Measure:

  • Capnography: Tracks carbon dioxide levels in breath to estimate respiratory rate.
  • Wearables: Devices with motion sensors for breath tracking.

6. Skin Temperature

Value Description: Changes in skin temperature can correlate with cardiovascular stress.

Tools to Measure:

  • Thermal Sensors in Wearables: Found in fitness trackers and smartwatches.
